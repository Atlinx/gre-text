Learning is a universal activity, with participants ranging from toddlers taking their first steps to college students studying for the GRE. People learn to acquire knowledge and skills, therefore the goal of teaching is to help learners acquire this knowledge as quickly as possible. The prompt argues that the best educational method is to solely praise positive behavior while ignoring negative ones. This approach can be detrimental to students' learning, and in reality, education requires a balanced approach that's tailored to the needs of the student.

An major obstacle to learning is understanding your knowledge and mistakes. If a student goes through an entire class without solving any problems, doing assignments or taking exams, they would not know if they've actually retained all the information that was taught. But by completing  exams and problem sets, a learner can use the scores they received to diagnose areas in need of improvement. Fixing mistakes early is also crucial to preparing students for more advanced subjects, since knowledge in a field builds upon itself. Consider a series of mathematic courses taught at an average college campus: The lowest-level course is Algebra, which teaches students variables, functions, graphs, and systems of equations. The next course is Calculus, which discusses the rate of change of functions, the area under curves on a graph, and differentiation. From Calculus comes multivariate-calculus, which extends the concepts of derivatives and integrals from Calculus to multi-variate functions. Within each course, a student is expected to have retained the knowledge learned from their previous courses. Not understanding how variables and functions work automatically precludes a student from learning calculus and multivariate-calculus. Therefore it's critical to catch these errors early so a student has a strong foundation to continue studying more advanced subjects in the future.
